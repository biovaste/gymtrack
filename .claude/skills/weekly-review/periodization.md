# Periodization Reference — Weekly Review Skill

Science underpinning the weekly planning decisions. Apply these principles; cite them when explaining reasoning to the user.

---

## A. Auto-Regulation via RPE (Load Progression)

**Principle:** Training load should be adjusted each week based on the athlete's actual performance response (RPE at a given load), not a fixed calendar percentage.

**Why RPE works:** RPE at a given load correlates with proximity to failure (reps in reserve). When an athlete performs a set at a lower RPE than planned, they have more reserve than expected — the load is too light to drive adaptation. When RPE is higher than planned, recovery was insufficient or the load is too heavy.

**Rules applied in this skill:**
- RPE < target by ≥ 0.5 → +2.5 kg: athlete recovered well, load can increase
- RPE at target (±0.5) → hold weight: optimal stimulus, seek more reps in the range
- RPE > target by ≥ 0.5 → hold or reduce: athlete was under-recovered or load too high

**Citations:**
- Zourdos, M.C. et al. (2016). Novel resistance training–specific RPE scale measuring repetitions in reserve. Journal of Strength and Conditioning Research, 30(1), 267–275.
- González-Badillo, J.J. & Sánchez-Medina, L. (2010). Movement velocity as a measure of loading intensity in resistance training. International Journal of Sports Medicine, 31(5), 347–352.

## B. Weekly Volume Landmarks

**Minimum Effective Volume (MEV):** The minimum weekly sets per muscle group that produces measurable adaptation.
**Maximum Adaptive Volume (MAV):** The volume that produces the most adaptation.
**Maximum Recoverable Volume (MRV):** The maximum volume the athlete can recover from in a week.

**Approximate landmarks (trained athletes, per muscle group per week):**

| Muscle group | MEV | MAV | MRV |
|---|---|---|---|
| Quads (squat pattern) | 6 sets | 10–16 sets | 16–22 sets |
| Glutes/hips (hinge pattern) | 4 sets | 8–14 sets | 14–20 sets |
| Chest/push | 6 sets | 10–20 sets | 18–24 sets |
| Back/pull | 8 sets | 14–22 sets | 22–30 sets |
| Shoulders | 8 sets | 12–20 sets | 20–26 sets |

**Application:** Flag if computed weekly sets from the plan fall below MEV for a primary muscle group; note if they approach MRV (risk of under-recovery). Use these as context, not hard rules — the athlete's RPE and readiness data override landmark estimates.

**Citation:** Israetel, M., Hoffman, J., & Smith, C.W. (2019). Scientific Principles of Strength Training. Renaissance Periodization.

## C. Deload Evidence

**Why deload:** Accumulated fatigue masks fitness gains. A well-timed deload allows supercompensation — the athlete comes back stronger than before the deload.

**When to deload:** Evidence-based protocols suggest a deload every 4–6 weeks for trained athletes, or earlier if performance metrics decline (VLA increase, CMJ drop, RPE inflation at fixed loads). The skill triggers a deload recommendation when ≥ 2 objective/subjective fatigue signals are present simultaneously.

**Deload parameters:**
- Volume: −40% (sufficient to reduce fatigue while maintaining neural drive)
- Intensity (weights): unchanged — moving the same weight at lower volume maintains the strength skill without adding fatigue
- Session count: reduce by 1–2 (drop lowest-priority sessions)
- Duration: 1 week is typically sufficient for trained athletes

**Citation:** Israetel, M. & Hoffman, J. (2017). How to Use Autoregulation for Strength and Hypertrophy. Renaissance Periodization.

## D. Neuromuscular Fatigue — CMJ as Readiness Proxy

**Principle:** Countermovement jump height is a sensitive marker of neuromuscular fatigue — it reflects the same explosive force production capacity required for heavy compound lifts and plyometrics.

**Thresholds:**
- CMJ drop ≥ 5% from baseline → meaningful neuromuscular fatigue signal (Gathercole et al., 2015)
- At a 30 cm baseline, 5% ≈ 1.5 cm; at a 36 cm baseline, 5% ≈ 1.8 cm
- Use 3 cm as a practical floor threshold when the user's baseline is unknown
- Intra-week trend (last CMJ − first CMJ) is the relevant signal for weekly planning; absolute CMJ is relevant for long-term progress tracking

**Companion signals:** Subjective energy, HRV, RHR elevation all correlate with CMJ-detected fatigue. When multiple signals align, confidence in the fatigue assessment increases.

**Citation:** Gathercole, R.J., Sporer, B., Stellingwerff, T., & Sleivert, G. (2015). Alternative countermovement-jump analysis to quantify acute neuromuscular fatigue. International Journal of Sports Physiology and Performance, 10(1), 84–92.

## E. Concurrent Training Interference

**The interference effect:** Endurance training performed in the same session or day as strength training can blunt strength and hypertrophy gains, primarily through molecular signalling interference (AMPK vs. mTOR pathways).

**Minimisation strategies (applied in scheduling):**
1. Separate strength and endurance sessions by ≥ 6 h when on the same day, or by 24 h on separate days
2. If same-day training is unavoidable: do strength first, endurance second
3. Endurance at low intensity (Zone 2) produces minimal interference — only high-intensity aerobic work interferes
4. Explosive sport (plyometrics, sprint-dominant, stunt/tumble) on the same day as heavy leg compound is the highest-risk combination — treat this like endurance interference

**In this skill:** High-demand sport sessions are treated as producing both fatigue and potential interference with the following 24 h. Schedule heavy compound sessions ≥ 24 h after explosive sport activity.

**Citations:**
- Wilson, J.M. et al. (2012). Concurrent training: A meta-analysis examining interference of aerobic and resistance exercises. Journal of Strength and Conditioning Research, 26(8), 2293–2307.
- Leveritt, M. et al. (1999). Concurrent strength and endurance training: A review. Sports Medicine, 28(6), 413–427.

## F. Polarized Aerobic Training Model

**Principle:** The optimal training intensity distribution for aerobic development is ~80% low-intensity (Zone 1–2, conversational) and ~20% high-intensity (Zone 4–5, near-maximal). Middle-intensity work ("threshold") produces disproportionate fatigue for its aerobic benefit in trained athletes.

**Application in weekly planning:**
- Low-intensity aerobic activity (bike commutes, easy runs, walks) accumulates naturally and costs little recovery
- The weekly plan needs only **one** genuinely hard aerobic session (e.g. 4×4 Norwegian intervals at ~90–95% HRmax) to drive VO2max improvement
- Adding a second hard aerobic session in the same week competes with strength/power recovery — schedule accordingly
- If sessions are limited, the aerobic-only day is always the first to drop

**Citation:** Seiler, K.S. & Kjerland, G.Ø. (2006). Quantifying training intensity distribution in elite endurance athletes: Is there evidence for an "optimal" distribution? Scandinavian Journal of Medicine and Science in Sports, 16(1), 49–56.
